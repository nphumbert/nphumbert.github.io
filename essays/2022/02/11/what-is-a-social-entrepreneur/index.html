Understanding Social Entrepreneurs: Motivations, Behaviours, and Impact

  • Nadia Zablah Humbert-Labeaumaz
  • February 11, 2022
  • 3 min read

Get the PDF

Enter your email to access this document.

Introduction

The concept of social entrepreneurship has expanded considerably over the past two decades, yet its definition remains unclear. This ambiguity can undermine the credibility of social entrepreneurs, weaken institutional support, and complicate impact measurement. Establishing a shared understanding is therefore essential for distinguishing social entrepreneurship from both traditional business and philanthropy.

This essay proposes a concise definition of social entrepreneurship and examines empirical evidence drawn from primary research conducted in Toronto, Canada, to identify recurring behavioural patterns and motivations across diverse contexts.

Defining Social Entrepreneurship

A social entrepreneur is an individual whose decisions are primarily guided by the objective of generating sustained social impact at scale, rather than by the pursuit of personal or shareholder profit.

This definition situates social entrepreneurship within the hybrid spectrum described by Alter, encompassing non-profit organizations that employ market-based mechanisms to sustain their mission while excluding for-profit entities where social responsibility remains peripheral.

Social entrepreneurship, therefore, represents an organizational logic in which social value creation drives strategic and operational choices, with economic performance serving as an enabler rather than an end in itself.

Characteristics of Social Entrepreneurs

Field research revealed several recurring characteristics among social entrepreneurs, suggesting a consistent behavioural profile across industries and organizational forms.

A Transformative Motivation

Most respondents reported formative experiences that shaped their social awareness and catalyzed their commitment to addressing specific societal challenges. Their ventures often emerged from lived experience and personal empathy, translating moral discomfort into pragmatic innovation.

Action Orientation and Pragmatism

Social entrepreneurs exhibit a strong executional drive. They prefer experimentation over extensive planning, adopting an iterative, learning by doing approach akin to lean methodologies. This orientation toward action compensates for resource scarcity and accelerates feedback cycles.

Dual Value Creation

While motivated by altruistic goals, social entrepreneurs also derive personal and psychological benefits from their work. These may include catharsis, fulfilment, and identity coherence, elements that sustain commitment over time and reduce the risk of burnout.

Risk Aversion and Resourcefulness

Contrary to popular perception, social entrepreneurs are not innate risk-takers. They tend to start modestly, mobilizing personal networks and limited resources before scaling gradually. This cautiousness reflects a strong sense of responsibility toward beneficiaries but can constrain growth when higher-risk capital is required.

Cognitive Openness

Participants demonstrated notable receptiveness to external input and learning, a trait essential for navigating uncertainty and complexity. However, this observation may partly reflect a selection bias, as willingness to participate in the study is itself indicative of openness.

Discussion

These findings align with prior literature identifying resilience, empathy, and pragmatic idealism as core attributes of social entrepreneurs. However, the observed risk aversion and emphasis on personal meaning add nuance to existing models.

Moreover, social entrepreneurs appear to embody a synthesis between entrepreneurial agency and social stewardship. Their ventures operate within the paradox of pursuing impact amid constraints, balancing innovation with accountability.

Understanding this balance is crucial for policymakers and investors seeking to design support mechanisms that honour social entrepreneurs’ distinctive motivations while facilitating scalability and sustainability.

Conclusion

Social entrepreneurship represents a hybrid form of value creation that merges business acumen with social purpose. Social entrepreneurs are not merely innovators in the market sense; they are catalysts of institutional change who redefine success in terms of social transformation.

Their cautious pragmatism, personal commitment, and resilience make them essential contributors to sustainable development. Yet, for their impact to reach full potential, the field must evolve toward clearer definitions, more adaptive funding structures, and wider recognition of social entrepreneurship as both a moral and managerial endeavour.
