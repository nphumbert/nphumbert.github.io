Continuous Improvement in the Digital Age

  • Nadia Zablah Humbert-Labeaumaz
  • July 13, 2026
  • 4 min read

Get the PDF

Enter your email to access this document.

Lean and continuous improvement are usually associated with factories. Indeed, Toyota developed many of the principles that still define continuous improvement in manufacturing today. Back then, work happened on factory floors, products moved along production lines, and waste was often visible. Teams could observe work directly, identify bottlenecks, and improve the system one problem at a time.

The context, however, has changed dramatically.

Much of today’s value creation no longer involves physical products. Instead, they involve decisions, information, software, and collaboration. Teams are also distributed across cities or continents. Work moves through Slack conversations, Jira tickets, GitHub repositories, CRMs, shared documents, and AI assistants.

At first glance, this makes traditional Lean practices seem less relevant. In practice, the principles have changed remarkably little. What has changed is how organizations create value.

The Workplace Has Become Digital

One of the clearest examples of this shift is Gemba.

Lean teaches that improvement begins by going to “the real place” where work is performed. For a software team, the Gemba is seldom a meeting room. It can be a pull request waiting for review, a support ticket bouncing between teams, a backlog that keeps on expanding, or Slack messages explaining why a decision was made.

The workplace has become digital, but the need to observe work directly has not disappeared.

In many ways, digital organizations are more observable than traditional ones. Work now flows through collaboration platforms, software repositories, customer systems, and AI-powered tools, leaving a digital trace almost everywhere.

That visibility is invaluable, but it has limits. Data explains what happened; it rarely explains why. A dashboard may show that approvals consistently take six days or that a feature spends half its lifecycle waiting for review. It cannot reveal whether the delay results from unclear priorities, fragmented ownership, competing workloads, or a decision that nobody feels empowered to make.

This is precisely why Gemba remains relevant. Going to the real place no longer means walking a production line. It means understanding the context in which the work happens by engaging with the people doing it.

Technology can identify patterns and surface anomalies. It can even suggest hypotheses. Understanding the system, however, still requires observation, discussion, and curiosity.

Waste Didn’t Disappear. It Changed.

If the workplace has changed, our understanding of waste must evolve with it.

  • Waiting now takes the form of delayed decisions, pending approvals, or features waiting to be deployed.
  • Transportation becomes information copied between systems.
  • Motion is now the constant context switching between meetings, emails, and messaging platforms.
  • Excess inventory takes the form of unmanaged backlogs, dormant projects, or knowledge that nobody can find.

Instead of asking people to work harder or faster, continuous improvement seeks to understand what prevents them from doing valuable work in the first place. The examples are different. The underlying principle hasn’t changed a bit.

Continuous Improvement Is Becoming Continuous Learning

The implications extend well beyond observation and waste elimination.

In the knowledge era, continuous improvement no longer focuses solely on processes. It also focuses on improving how organizations learn.

Technology changes continuously, and AI is transforming the way knowledge work is performed. In this environment, the objective is no longer to design the perfect process but to build dynamic capabilities that enable organizations to refine their processes continuously.

The competitive advantage no longer belongs to the organization with the best process. It belongs to the organization that learns, adapts, and improves faster than everyone else.

The Mindset Still Matters

Yet despite these transformations, one thing has remained remarkably consistent.

Continuous improvement was never about factory floors, Kanban boards, or production lines. Those were simply the environments in which its principles first emerged.

Its lasting contribution is a way of thinking: observe reality, understand the system, solve the underlying problem, measure the results, and learn from the outcome.

Technology will continue to evolve, and so will the tools. The mindset that connects them remains as valuable today as it was decades ago.
